My Projects

FAST and AVOD Revamp - Plex

Overview

As the lead UX strategist and researcher across Plex’s Streaming Content, Personal Media, and Playback teams, I led a full-platform UX initiative to overhaul both FAST (Free Ad-Supported Streaming TV) and AVOD (Ad-Supported Video on Demand) experiences.

This dual-track effort spanned mobile, web, and connected TV platforms. I conducted deep-dive usability studies, platform audits, competitive analyses, and stakeholder workshops to unify strategy and define a scalable, user-centered experience model for both FAST and AVOD content.

The outcome: A prioritized roadmap rooted in both business constraints and user behavior, aligned with design system and engineering feasibility.

Target Users

  • For FAST: General users (with and without accounts), PMS users with tuners
  • For AVOD: General, non-PMS users (with and without accounts)

Key Contributions

  • UX analysis and heuristic audits across FAST and AVOD on iOS, Android, Web (Watch/App), Roku, Fire TV, and LG/Lightning
  • Metadata and taxonomy standardization for multi-format content
  • Research planning and synthesis: user personas, usability studies (150+ participants), and JTBD frameworks
  • Competitive analysis across 15+ streaming platforms (both direct and indirect)
  • Low-fidelity wireframes, flow maps, and prioritization matrices to align design, product, and engineering teams
  • Accessibility evaluations and implementation recommendations across platforms

Main Challenges

Inconsistent Flows

Each TV platform had slightly different UI architecture, navigation logic, and flow interruptions, making it difficult to identify a universal path. I documented and aligned platform-specific inconsistencies to build standard flow models that still respected technical constraints.

Fragmented Metadata

Content types (movies, series, AVOD/FAST formats) had diverging taxonomy rules, naming conventions, and layout inconsistencies, sometimes even within the same platform. I performed metadata audits and wireframed cross-system proposals for streamlining and reclassification

Roadmap & Engineering Overlap

The AVOD and FAST roadmaps were being developed in tandem by engineering, each in different stages of completion. My research efforts needed to remain nimble, continually reshaping deliverables to accommodate shifting timelines and interdependencies.

Full Process

Requirements Gathering

Both projects began with a pretty ambiguous prompt from my product managers: “Hey, let’s fix FAST/AVOD.” So, my first step was clarifying the scope: figuring out the who, what, when, where, and why behind the ask. I set up stakeholder meetings to align on the specific problems they were trying to solve, the metrics they wanted to move, target users (if any), and the expected timeline.

  • The main goals were to fix two key issues: first, the lack of consistency across platforms (iOS, Android, watch.plex, app.plex, Fire TV, Roku, LG/Lightning), and second, the overall usability of the FAST experience. I began by mapping out what was actually happening in each environment, comparing it to best practices in the industry, and identifying what a consistent and scalable baseline could look like. From there, I started identifying areas of improvement for future iterations.
  • Many of the same baseline problems applied, but the focus shifted from Plex Media Server users to general streaming users. The goal here was less about consistency and more about registration and retention. That distinction shaped how I approached the research and prioritized improvements.

Non-User Research

After aligning with stakeholders, I moved into non-user/market research to ground the projects in existing internal knowledge and external best practices. My goals were threefold: identify internal documentation or design artifacts, walk through the application across target platforms to map flow and consistency issues, and analyze competitors for metadata formatting, structure, and overall AVOD/FAST patterns. The following analyses helped to figure these out.

Metadata Analysis and Usability Analysis:

To address the cross-platform consistency issue, I conducted a full metadata and usability analysis of both FAST and AVOD flows. This began with exhaustive walkthroughs of each major page across key platforms. I wireframed out each flow, paying close attention to how metadata was displayed, formatted, and interacted with across environments. These wireframes allowed me to surface inconsistencies in layout, functionality, and user expectations, forming the basis for actionable recommendations on how to unify and improve them moving forward.

Competitive Analysis:

I conducted parallel research on how competitors handled metadata and flow structure. For AVOD, I maintained a list of 11 platforms (5 direct competitors (free ad-supported services) and 6 indirect (paid streaming platforms). For FAST, I reviewed between 10–15 competitors, depending on feature alignment and use case fit. I focused my analyses on three key areas:

  • Metadata formatting conventions across platforms
  • Structural layout and page flow of AVOD/FAST experiences
  • Navigation hierarchy and organization standards in the space

This competitive scan helped triangulate best practices and gave me a strategic lens to assess where Plex’s current implementations stood and where we could lead.

User Research

With a strong foundation of internal and market data, I created a usability research plan focused on real-world behaviors across FAST and AVOD platforms. The goal was to better understand user engagement, content consumption habits, and pain points with metadata presentation and navigation.

For the FAST study

Target users were non-PMS (personal media server) viewers who watched at least 1–2 hours of live TV weekly, regardless of platform. The study spanned mobile, web, and TV platforms. Participants were asked to walk through their typical usage of FAST, narrating what they liked, disliked, or found confusing. At the end, they answered questions about their behaviors, viewing patterns, and general expectations.

  • Participants: ~80 recruited
  • Usable responses: 40–50 analyzed
  • Method: Unmoderated sessions via UserTesting.com

For the AVOD study

Similar to the FAST study, AVOD research targeted non-PMS users watching 1–2+ hours of AVOD content weekly. This group also spanned mobile, web, and TV platforms. Sessions asked participants to explore the AVOD experience as they naturally would, then respond to structured follow-up questions to better understand discoverability, retention behavior, and expectations.

  • Participants: ~110 recruited
  • Usable responses: 60 analyzed
  • Method: Unmoderated sessions via UserTesting.com

Recommendations and Brainstorming

With all research synthesized, I delivered a comprehensive findings report that included detailed breakdowns of platform inconsistencies, usability challenges, and content engagement patterns, alongside JTBD personas developed for both FAST and AVOD, as requested by the product team.

Following the synthesis presentation, I facilitated a collaborative brainstorming workshop with key stakeholders to generate and prioritize new feature ideas for both FAST and AVOD. Ideas were mapped in a prioritization matrix to evaluate impact vs. effort and funneled into a UX strategy backlog. This evolving backlog now serves as the foundation for upcoming research, iteration, and design cycles across both flows.
